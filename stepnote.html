Work About Resume

Stepnote

Winter 2015 · Stanford, CA

Overview

Choreographers and dancers are always listening and brainstorming, but the process of developing a new dance routine has room for improvement. Stepnote is a web application created to help choreographers both record and recall their ideas. Stepnote was developed end-to-end from user interviews all the way to a working prototype.

Role

Created low-fidelity prototypes and ran user testing. Contributed to interaction design and front-end development.

Timeline

4 weeks

Teammates

Ishita Prasad, Tulsee Doshi

Methods

User research, User interviews, Ideation, Personas, User testing, Prototyping, UX/UI design, Front-end development

Context

The Data Narratives of Choreographers

The process of choreographing a dance routine is complex. First, choreographers must choose the music, often mixing and mashing different songs for a routine. Then, they both take in and put out a lot of information, consuming inspiration and moves from YouTube videos and other dances to produce the steps and formations of a new routine. Finally, they practice, often sharing their choreography with others to learn. Information and data play huge roles in a choreographer's process.

Dance routines must be carefully choreographed and constructed.

Problem

Understanding Choreographers' Behavior

Focusing in on designing a tool for choreography, we first talked to our main stakeholders: the choreographers themselves. Meeting with a wide range of choreographers from classical Indian dance to hip hop, we wanted to understand how choreographers chose music, looked for inspiration, and constructed their dance routines.

Two things were clear and evident by the end of our interviews. First, the process behind choreography varies person to person and is often unique, with some users taking detailed notes with timestamps and others taking minmal or no notes at all. Second, every choreographer struggled to recall things, whether it was an inspiring dance move from a YouTube video, or the latest set of steps they chose when they last sat down to choreograph.

To condense the diverse set of skill levels, behaviors, and attitudes of the choreographers we talked to, we created three user personas to design for.

    Freestyle Freddie

    Freddie is a creative when it comes to his choreography, and he picks music that really "speaks to him." He gleans inspiration from the melodies and rhythms of the music, and relies on his mental notes.

    Structured Shilpa

    Shilpa records all her steps and ideas in her process, often taking videos of her movements. She relies on a specific dictionary of steps, meaning she is often limited to only the steps that come to mind.

    Novice Nancy

    Nancy is still learning choreography&emdash;she relies heavily on videos of other dances for inspiration. She often finds exciting steps, but has trouble incorporating them into her routines.

Approach

Needfinding and Ideation

With a struggle to recall ideas being the main gap in our users' behavior, we brainstormed some concepts to help users remember their sources of inspiration and the dance moves that they were choreographing. Some concepts focused on splicing existing moves from YouTube videos, while others helped choreographers understand their repetoire of steps.

Before long, we had a few ideas to explore.

Iteration

User Studies

Moving forward, we decided to stick with our best idea, Song Annotator—the idea was feasible to prototype and test in three weeks, and it seemed to resonate best with the choreographers we interviewed. Now there remained two questions to answer. What screen size would we develop for, web or mobile? And what format of annotations would we support: text, audio, or video?

We quickly moved to low-fidelity paper prototypes, testing the possible combinations of screen size and annotation format with multiple choreographers. Before, long, we had chosen a web application that would support text based annotations.

We explored our questions quickly through Wizard of Oz prototypes.

Testing the Interface

As we began to develop our idea into a working prototype, questions about the user interface and interactions began to arise.

    Would users prefer to annotate moments or ranges?

    With timestamp-linked annotations, one of our implementation questions was enabling users to annotate either specific moments or longer ranges in the music. While users appreciated the flexibility of annotating ranges, annotating singular moments was less confusing overall.

    What time should be used for an annotation's timestamp?

    There are two options here: once a user starts to type their note, or once a user enters and submits their annotation. Our users preferred the former, as mistakes while typing could shift their annotation timestamp in the latter case.

    What might users make annotations about?

    Users made notes and ideas on everything: references to YouTube videos, ideas for different steps, or possible formations for the group. We noticed that users often worked within specific categories of annotations.

Solution

Stepnote, the Song Annotator

We built our web application using HTML, CSS, and vanilla JavaScript. Before long, we had our working prototype, which you can explore. You can also see the poster for our presentation.

A screenshot of the final version of our prototype.

Here's a quick run-down of Stepnote's main features:

    Simple waveform visualization and playback

    Looking towards SoundCloud for inspiration, we leveraged a JavaScript library to integrate a visual waveform of the music being choreographed for. Users appreciated the simple playback controls and overlayed annotations.

    Lightweight interface for ease of use

    The interface isn't complex, and it makes a lot of sense: you can use your spacebar to pause and play music just like most apps, and annotations are highlighted as the music play.

    Easy annotations with timestamps and motifs

    Timestamp-linked annotations mean that users don't have to pause, check, and manually enter the time each time they want to note something. And motifs offer a functionality akin to hashtags for grouping notes.

Impact

Reception and Feedback

Choreographers loved Stepnote, and our teaching staff loved our process and output. Users described our prototype as "surprisingly natural," "sick," and "a dream." The idea was well-received and we received an honorable mention at our final presentations. Looking toward the future, our users saw potential in our product, and with more time, we would've loved to add multi-user annotation and shareable links for collaboration.
